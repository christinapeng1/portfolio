    A Reimagination of HowToStudyKorean dot com

    Posted on March 13, 2025

    How it all Started

    How to Study Korean is a free language-learning website for people learning Korean. It offers free lessons, workbooks, audio tutorials, and more. I frequented this website over winter break when I was trying to learn Korean. The lessons were comprehensive, and I loved the personal anecdotes 선생님 (teacher) would include to make the learning more digestible.

    While I found the content incredibly valuable, the site's design felt outdated. The navigation wasn't as intuitive as it could be for beginners trying to stay motivated. I saw this as a great opportunity to reimagine the home page with a more modern, user-friendly approach that keeps the charm of the original site while improving usability.

    I created a final prototype of the redesign, which you can check out here.

    Usability Problems

    First, I looked for problems with the current website to understand what needed to be improved. My analysis is based on four criteria: efficiency, learnability, memorability, and conceptual model:

    Criterion Identified Problems
    Efficiency - Cluttered homepage makes it difficult to find key sections.
    - Hidden search bar limits quick navigation.
    - Have to click through multiple layers to visit a lesson.
    Learnability - Unclear user journey and limited guidance for new users.
    - Difficult for new users to understand what to click on first.
    Memorability - No persistent navigation cues or progress tracking.
    - Users returning after a break may have difficulty remembering which lesson they left off at.
    Conceptual Model - The site layout does not align with standard learning platforms.
    - Lessons are grouped in numerical ranges instead of individually segmented by topic.

    Together, these issues make it harder for learners to stay focused, find content quickly, or return to where they left off.

    Accessibility Problems

    Additionally, I ran the website through WebAIM Wave, a web accessibility evaluation tool for people with disabilities. Here's what it found:

    Issue Details
    Missing alternative text - 5 linked images have no alt attributes
    - Limited screen reader access for images.
    Low contrast text - 57 very low contrast errors
    - Several text elements (especially links) don’t meet WCAG contrast ratio standards.
    Empty links - 2 empty links
    - Some links are visually present but contain no readable text.
    Redundant links and headings - 4 redundant links
    - 2 redundant title text
    - 1 skipped heading level
    - Inconsistent heading levels and redundant links create confusion for screen readers and assistive tools.
    Suspicious link or alternative text - 1 suspicious link text; contains extraneous text or may not make sense out of context.
    - 1 suspicious alternative text; does not provide the same information conveyed by the image.

    You can view the full report here.

    I think most of the issues flagged by WAVE are valid and highlight key problems in the site's accessibility. Missing alt text and low-contrast text make it difficult for users with visual impairments. Structural issues—like empty or improperly nested headings—disrupt the page's semantic flow, hindering screen reader navigation and overall usability.

    Initial Sketches

    To kick off the redesign process, I started with a few concept sketches:

    While sketching the redesigned site, these were my goals:

    • Make lessons directly accessible from the home page
    • Highlight the site's search functionality
    • Add a direct user path for new users coming to the site
    • Make it easier for returning users to pick up where they left off

    High-Fidelity Mockups

    Next, I transformed my sketches into high-fidelity mockups for varying screen sizes. Using Figma, I made mockups of my designs for Desktop, Tablet, and Mobile:

    The goal of my mockups was to think through how the structure and flow of information should shift depending on the screen size. Here's a summary of my key design choices:

    • The original color scheme (red, white, black) and overall structure are retained across all breakpoints.
    • Top navigation is replaced with a vertical left nav for a textbook-style layout.
    • A persistent CTA button ("Begin from..."/"Continue from...") helps users resume learning. Enlarges and turns red on hover.
    • “Additional Resources” are converted into tappable cards to drive interaction.
    • Left nav becomes a hamburger menu on tablet/mobile.
    • On smaller screens, the search bar moves to the top and expands to emphasize search functionality.

    Development

    Click here to view the final redesign!

    The images below showcase the redesign across three screen sizes:

    • Mobile – 430 × 932 (iPhone Pro Max)
    • Tablet – 820 × 1180 (iPad Air)
    • Desktop – 2560 × 1664

    I made the site using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. I implemented a custom hamburger menu for tablet and mobile navigation, making the experience smoother and more intuitive on smaller screens. I also tested different font sizes and language settings to ensure the layout remains readable and functional, even when content scales.

    The redesign addresses earlier usability concerns—like cluttered layout and confusing navigation—by introducing clearer visual hierarchy, better spacing, and more prominent calls to action. It also addresses accessibility concerns by increasing text contrast, ensuring all links are descriptive, and adding meaningful alt text to images. Together, these changes make the site not only more intuitive to navigate but also more inclusive for users with diverse needs.

    Conclusion

    Redesigning How to Study Korean was both a fun and meaningful challenge. It pushed me to think critically about usability, accessibility, and the nuances of responsive design. From auditing the original interface to building high-fidelity mockups and developing a fully functional, responsive page, I learned how even small design decisions—like button placement or visual hierarchy—can significantly impact the user experience.

    Ultimately, this project is a reflection of my approach to design: content-first, user-centered, and accessibility-aware. I hope this reimagination makes the site feel more approachable for new learners while supporting the needs of returning users. Thanks for following along!
